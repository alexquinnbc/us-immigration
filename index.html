üìö Legal Pathways Explained

This section explains the main ways individuals can become Lawful Permanent Residents (LPRs), also known as getting a "Green Card". The U.S. grants up to 675,000 permanent immigrant visas annually, although some categories have no limits.

Family-Based Immigration

This is the cornerstone of the U.S. system (58% of new LPRs in FY 2022). The process usually starts with a U.S. Citizen or LPR filing Form I-130.

  • Immediate Relatives of U.S. Citizens: NO ANNUAL CAPS. Includes spouses, unmarried children under 21, and parents of adult citizens.
  • Family Preference Categories: SUBJECT TO ANNUAL LIMITS and long waits. Includes adult children, married children, and siblings of U.S. Citizens, and spouses/children of LPRs.

Employment-Based Immigration

For immigrants with skills valuable to the U.S. economy (140,000 visas per year cap).

  • EB-1: Extraordinary ability, outstanding professors, multinational executives.
  • EB-2: Advanced degrees or exceptional ability.
  • EB-3: Skilled workers, professionals.
  • EB-4 & EB-5: Special immigrants and investors.

Humanitarian Protection

For those with a "well-founded fear of persecution" due to race, religion, nationality, social group, or political opinion. Eligible for LPR status after one year.

  • Refugees: Apply from outside the U.S.
  • Asylees: Apply from inside the U.S. or at a port of entry.
  • CAUTION: Asylum access is subject to extreme and frequent policy changes. See our Policy Alerts and seek legal help immediately.
  • Other Relief (TPS, DACA): These are temporary and DO NOT automatically lead to a Green Card.

Diversity Visa Program (The "Lottery")

A random lottery for 55,000 visas annually for individuals from countries with low rates of immigration to the U.S.

‚öñÔ∏è Know Your Rights

The following is GENERAL information about constitutional rights, NOT legal advice. Always consult an attorney.

You have the right to remain silent.

If an immigration (ICE, CBP) or police officer asks you questions, you can say, "I wish to remain silent and speak to an attorney." You do not have to answer questions about your immigration status.

If an Agent Comes to Your Home:

  • Do not open your door.
  • Ask them to show you a warrant signed by a judge through a window or under the door.
  • You have the right to remain silent through the closed door.

If You Are Stopped in a Car or Public Place:

  • Ask "Am I free to leave?" If yes, calmly leave.
  • You can say, "I do not consent to a search."
  • Do not provide false documents. It is better to provide nothing and remain silent.

Understanding the Visa Bulletin

The Visa Bulletin is the government's waiting list for green cards in capped categories. Your "Priority Date" is your place in line. You must wait for the "Final Action Date" on the chart to pass your priority date before a green card can be issued.

The "Per-Country Ceiling" (7% cap per country) can cause much longer waits for people from high-demand countries like India, China, Mexico, and the Philippines.

POLICY ALERT: ASYLUM PATHWAYS

üìñ Glossary of Terms

  • LPR (Lawful Permanent Resident): A "Green Card" holder.
  • USCIS (U.S. Citizenship and Immigration Services): The agency that handles petitions.
  • INA (Immigration and Nationality Act): The main body of U.S. immigration law.
  • Priority Date: Your place in the waiting line for a visa.
  • Visa Bulletin: The monthly publication showing who is at the front of the line.
  • Immediate Relative: Spouse, minor child, or parent of a U.S. citizen. Not subject to visa caps.
  • Adjustment of Status: Applying for a Green Card from within the U.S.
  • Consular Processing: Applying for a visa/Green Card from an embassy outside the U.S.
